🎙️ EDH BROADCAST — GAME NIGHT DISPATCH

WELCOME, PLANESWALKERS, TO THE INAUGURAL SHUFFLE — the very first game night in the official MTGCapsul record books!


Six players shuffled into the pod tonight: Adam, Bryan, Jordan, Rich, Shake, and Waid. The crew brought everything from tribal synergy engines to infinite combo packages to a literal Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles precon. Five games. Three different formats. One absolutely unhinged Hermit Druid milling an entire library into a graveyard kill shot.


The night opened with Bryan's Szarel taking Game 1 and Rich's Kiki-Jiki going infinite in Game 2 — "But I asked permission!" has already become the pod's first catchphrase. The middle games saw a chaotic 3-team Two-Headed Giant and a Star variant where Bryan's Muldrotha performed actual necromancy, milling 90+ cards and using Jarad + Lord of Extinction to nuke the entire table from the graveyard.


Adam closed the night with a comeback win piloting Gilanra — ironic, given that Bryan's Kardur accidentally superpowered Adam into four combat phases with double damage. When your goad commander accidentally creates a Timmy's dream scenario for the opponent, you've earned that L.


Five games. Six players. Two formats we'd never tried before. The meta is wide open and the salt is flowing. Game night is officially a thing.

The very first game in the official record books — and Bryan's Szarel wastes no time establishing dominance. The Jund value engine started generating tokens, pumping them, and swinging in while the rest of the table was still figuring out their mana bases. Adam's Ziatora got a few sacrifice triggers in, Rich's Scarab God started reanimating threats, and Jordan's Eriette was locking down creatures with enchantments — but none of it was fast enough to outpace Szarel's relentless board presence.

The lesson from Game 1? Fabled Passage is a kill-on-sight target. The table learned this the hard way.

📝 "ALWAYS EXILE FABLED PASSAGE IMMEDIATELY. kthxbai"

An hour and eight minutes of incremental board development, careful threat assessment, and political maneuvering — all rendered completely irrelevant by one card and one ETB trigger.

Rich went infinite with Kiki-Jiki. The mono-red combo legend found his untap piece, started copying it, and created approximately one billion hasty token copies to swing lethal at the entire table simultaneously. Adam had a Turn 4 Marit Lage — a 20/20 flying indestructible black token — and it did absolutely nothing because infinite combat damage doesn't care about your legendary 20/20.

The salt hit 3 on this one. Not because of the combo itself — it's a clean kill. But because Rich looked the whole table in the eye and said "But I asked permission!" before going off. The first catchphrase of the pod is born.

📝 "Rich wins via infinite combo w/ Kiki-Jiki (But I asked permission!) Turn 4 Marit Lage did nothing. (adam)"

Ten minutes and forty-two seconds. That's how long three-team Two-Headed Giant lasted. Six players, three teams, and the game was over before most Commander pods finish their third land drop.

Team 1 (Bryan's Jetmir + Adam's Mighty Myrs) combined wide board presence with Jetmir's "creatures you control get +1/+0 and trample" anthem stacking and just... ran over the other two teams before they could stabilize. Team 2 (Jordan's Atraxa + Rich's Kaalia) and Team 3 (Waid's Squirrels + Shake's Leonardo TMNT) barely got to develop their boards.

Zero salt. Hard to be salty about a game that was over before you finished your first beer.

📝 "3-team 2HG. Bryan+Adam (T1) vs Jordan+Rich (T2) vs Waid+Shake (T3). Team 1 wins."

First-ever Star variant in the pod. Five players, you can only attack (and can only be attacked by) the two players across from you. Kill both your enemies to win. Political alliances. Hidden agendas. And then Bryan's Muldrotha happened.

Here's what went down: Bryan activated Hermit Druid — the classic "reveal cards until you hit a basic land" self-mill engine. Problem: Bryan's deck had no basic lands. So the Druid just... milled his entire library into the graveyard. The whole thing. Every card.

And from that 90+ card graveyard, Bryan assembled Jarad, Golgari Lich Lord plus Lord of Extinction — a creature whose power and toughness equal the number of cards in all graveyards. With 90+ cards in the yard, Lord of Extinction was a 90/90. Jarad sacrificed it, dealing 90+ damage to every opponent simultaneously.

The whole table just sat there. Five players eliminated in one activation. Star variant over. Bryan's Muldrotha didn't just win the game — it performed actual necromancy by turning an empty library into a kill shot that would make a Yu-Gi-Oh protagonist blush.

📝 "Bryan's Hermit Druid failed to find, causing bryan to perform miracles to somehow avoid death by casting from the now whole deck graveyard. Jarad and Lord of Extinction. Doing 90+ damage to all of us at once. wtf. holy shit. *"

The night closer — and the best comedy of errors in Game Night 1.

Bryan brought out Kardur Doomscourge (the goad commander) for its debut, forcing the table to swing at each other while he played political puppeteer. Jordan's Xenagos was doubling creature power and slamming face. Shake ran back the TMNT precon. Waid deployed The Wise Mothman for some Rad counter shenanigans. And Adam? Adam was quietly ramping with Gilanra, building mana, waiting.

Then Bryan played Kratos — yes, the God of War Secret Lair card — targeting Adam. Instead of weakening a threat, it gave Adam's creatures a massive combat boost. Suddenly Adam had four combat phases in one turn with double damage. Gilanra's big green board went absolutely nuclear. Four swings. Double damage each time. Table eliminated.

Bryan accidentally created a monster. The pod's first own-goal. Adam takes the W and closes out Game Night 1 with style — even if the style was "my opponent accidentally gave me god mode."

📝 "Bryan fucked up. He played Kratos and superpowered Adam into having a turn where he got 4 combats and double damage."
